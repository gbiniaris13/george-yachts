# Greece Enters 2027 With Record Tourism, EUR 260 Million in New Marina Investment, and a Charter Market Poised to Rebound

## George Yachts publishes its 2027 Greek Luxury Charter Outlook, reading the case for a recovery year against the data: record arrivals, a wave of luxury openings, and infrastructure spend that is reshaping where superyachts can berth.

**KIFISIA, ATHENS, Greece, [DATE] 2026** — Greece is heading into the 2027 charter season from a position of strength that few Mediterranean destinations can match. After a softer 2026 for motor-yacht demand, weighed down by elevated fuel costs and geopolitical caution, the structural signals now point toward recovery. George Yachts, the boutique Forbes-featured crewed charter brokerage, today publishes its 2027 Greek Luxury Charter Outlook, drawing on the firm's own Greek Charter Index together with the latest national tourism and infrastructure data.

The headline is simple. The demand base has never been larger, the supply of luxury experience is expanding fast, and the berths that hold the largest yachts are finally being built.

### Record demand sets the floor

Greece closed 2025 with EUR 23.63 billion in tourism receipts, up 9.4 percent year on year, and 37.98 million international arrivals, both all-time records confirmed by the Bank of Greece. [1] Tourism investment held above EUR 5 billion per year across 2024 and 2025. [1] For the segment George Yachts serves, the foundation matters more than the headline: by the firm's own Greek Charter Index, roughly 30 percent of Mediterranean summer charter bookings take place in Greek waters, making Greece the single most popular charter destination in the region.

That popularity is concentrated where it counts. Across the wider charter market, the Eastern Mediterranean, Western Mediterranean and Adriatic accounted for almost 80 percent of forward June bookings, with Athens the leading embarkation point. [2]

### A softer 2026, and the case for a 2027 rebound

George Yachts does not overstate the present. Motor-yacht demand softened materially in 2026, pressured by war and elevated fuel prices, and the firm expects pent-up demand to return for the 2027 season. The external booking data is consistent with a market pausing rather than retreating: average charter booking lead times fell from 118 days in 2025 to 83 days in 2026, a contraction of almost 30 percent. Industry analysts attribute the shorter window to geopolitical caution and a wait-and-see approach to forward planning, rather than a lasting change in how clients charter. [2]

For 2027, that caution is the opportunity. Mykonos and Santorini lead 2026 demand on the Greek Charter Index, with Milos the fastest-rising island. Typical lead time for peak July and August is 6 to 12 months, and premium 40-metre-plus yachts are reserved a year or more ahead, so the clients who commit early to 2027 will hold the best of a tightening fleet.

### The luxury supply curve is bending upward

The experience layer that supports UHNW charter guests on land is expanding in step with demand. Mandarin Oriental, Athens is scheduled to open in summer 2027 on the coastal front of The Ellinikon development, with 123 rooms and suites and 17 branded residences, positioned on the Athenian Riviera near the Agios Kosmas Marina with direct access to the sea. [3] For charter clients, a marquee arrival of this kind anchors the pre- and post-cruise stay precisely where many Greek itineraries begin.

### The berths are finally being built

The most consequential shift for the largest yachts is infrastructure. Greece has long been described as Europe's laggard in five-star marinas, a gap that constrained where superyachts could berth. That is changing on two fronts.

At national level, the government has announced a EUR 260 million program to upgrade 30 island ports and expand marina infrastructure, with EUR 180 million from the NSRF "Transport 2021-2027" program and EUR 80 million from the Recovery and Resilience Fund, alongside a planned maritime-tourism hub in the Ionian. [4] At project level, plans have been unveiled for a EUR 524 million redevelopment of the Port of Platygiali at Astakos into what backers describe as the largest mega-yacht marina and resort in the Mediterranean, spanning more than 150 hectares with a 350-berth superyacht marina, a luxury hotel, villas and leisure residences. [5]

Together these commitments address the one structural constraint that has held Greece below its potential at the top of the market: somewhere for the biggest yachts to stay.

### What it means for 2027

"The data tells a clear story. Demand for Greece has never been deeper, the luxury offer on land is expanding, and for the first time the country is building berths worthy of the yachts our clients charter," said George P. Biniaris, Founder and Managing Broker of George Yachts. "2026 was a year of caution. We read 2027 as a year of return. The clients who move early will charter the best of a fleet that tightens as confidence comes back."

George Yachts curates approximately 67 crewed motor yachts, sailing yachts and catamarans across the Cyclades, the Ionian, the Saronic and the Dodecanese. By the firm's rate card, an all-in week aboard a crewed sailing catamaran for twelve guests runs a net base of EUR 15,000 to EUR 40,000 in 2026 peak season, while a 30-metre motor yacht is roughly EUR 117,000 all-in for a peak-season week. All-in means base plus APA of around 33 percent, VAT at the reduced 13 percent rate applied to commercial charters over 48 hours, and gratuity of around 17.5 percent, quoted in full and in advance.
